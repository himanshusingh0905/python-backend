Limits — "What are you approaching?"

A limit asks: as x gets closer and closer to some value, where does f(x) seem to be heading? You care about the journey, not whether you actually arrive.

Simple definition
limx→a f(x) = L means: the closer x gets to a, the closer f(x) gets to L. Even if f(a) is undefined or messy at the exact point.
Physical analogy
Your car's speedometer shows 60 km/h. But "speed at an exact instant" means zero distance in zero time — 0/0 makes no sense. Limits are how we define it anyway by squeezing time intervals smaller and smaller.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR ML
Derivatives in ML are literally defined using limits. When you compute how much the loss changes for a tiny change in weights, you are computing limΔw→0 [L(w+Δw) − L(w)] / Δw. Without limits, backpropagation has no mathematical foundation.
Key insight
sin(x)/x is undefined at x=0 (you'd get 0/0). But the limit as x→0 is exactly 1. The function has a "hole" at x=0, yet it has a perfectly clear limit.
When limit doesn't exist
If the function approaches different values from the left vs right side of a point, the limit doesn't exist. Like a cliff — coming from land or sea gives different "heights".

Continuity — "Can you draw it without lifting your pen?"

A function is continuous at a point if: the limit exists there, the function is defined there, and both values actually agree. No jumps, no holes, no gaps.

Simple definition
f is continuous at x=a if limx→a f(x) = f(a). The value the function is approaching and the actual value at that point must be the same thing.
Physical analogy
Imagine driving on a road. A continuous road = smooth, no potholes, no sudden teleportations. A discontinuous road = suddenly you're 2 metres to the left, or the road disappears entirely.
Compare different types of functions
WHY IT MATTERS FOR ML
Neural network activation functions must be continuous (mostly). If your function jumps, your gradient signal breaks. Sigmoid, tanh, ReLU are all continuous. Step functions (used in old-school perceptrons) are not — which is exactly why they were replaced.
Three conditions
f is continuous at a if: (1) f(a) is defined, (2) the limit exists at a, (3) they're equal. Fail any one condition → discontinuous.
Real world example
Temperature over time is continuous — it can't jump from 20°C to 50°C instantly. But a tax bracket system is discontinuous — earn ₹1 more and suddenly your rate changes by a step.

Differentiability — "Is it smooth enough to have a unique slope?"

A function is differentiable at a point if you can draw exactly one tangent line there — no sharp corners, no vertical walls. Differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity.

Simple definition
f is differentiable at x=a if the derivative exists there: the function is smooth enough that you can define a unique slope (tangent line) at that exact point. No kinks.
Physical analogy
A smooth rolling hill is differentiable everywhere — you can always find the slope at any point. A mountain with a sharp peak is not differentiable at the tip — from left you'd measure one slope, from right a different one.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR ML — THIS IS THE BIG ONE
Gradient descent needs derivatives. If f is not differentiable at a point, gradient descent breaks down there. This is why we obsess over smooth activation functions. ReLU is technically not differentiable at 0 — we handle it by convention (subgradient). Smooth alternatives like GELU, Swish, and Mish were invented specifically to fix this.
The strict rule
Differentiable → Continuous. Always. But Continuous ≠ Differentiable. |x| is continuous everywhere but has a kink at x=0 where you can't define a unique slope.
What the derivative actually is
The derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line at that point. That slope tells you: if x increases by a tiny bit, by how much does f(x) change? That's your gradient.

How the three concepts connect

They form a hierarchy. Each stronger condition implies the weaker ones — but not the other way around.

The implication chain
Differentiable
Smooth slope everywhere
⟹

⟹
Limit exists

But the reverse is NOT guaranteed

Limit exists, but not continuous
f(x) = sin(x)/x — limit exists at x=0 (equals 1), but f(0) is undefined. So not continuous. The limit "works" but the function has a hole.
Continuous, but not differentiable
f(x) = |x| — perfectly continuous everywhere, including x=0. But at x=0 there's a sharp corner. Left slope = −1, right slope = +1. No unique tangent. Not differentiable there.
THE ML SUMMARY
Your tutor is teaching limits → continuity → differentiability in that order because each concept builds on the one before. In ML, you want your model functions to be differentiable (so gradient descent works), which means they must also be continuous (so no jumps in the loss surface), which means limits must exist everywhere (so the math is well-defined). Every activation function debate in deep learning — sigmoid vs tanh vs ReLU vs GELU — is secretly a debate about these three properties.
